David Letterman is well known for his dry, often goofy, and regularly self-deprecating brand of humor. But, the truth is that Dave is among some of the country’s all-time most influential and famous comedians. In 2012, Dave was honored among a small group of talented individuals when he was awarded the Kennedy Center Honor.

“I’m thrilled for my family. They’re looking at me like, ‘He’s not this dumbass now.’”

- David Letterman accepting the Kennedy Center Honors (1)

The Kennedy Center Honors advisory committee felt differently when they awarded Dave this honor. The rainbow ribbon symbolizes the many skills within the performing arts and the spectrum of individuals that have received the award. The Kennedy Center Honor is one of the nation’s highest awards given to individuals. Dave was recognized for his lifetime contributions to American culture with his 33 years of hosting late night television shows.

The rainbow colors on the Kennedy Center Honor represent the many skills within the performing arts. Started in 1978, the Kennedy Center Honors is one of the most respected awards given to performers. Dave’s shows helped launch the careers of many comedians and musicians. And by interviewing many political leaders over the years, Dave also brought attention to important topics such as world hunger and climate change.

Recognized for significant contributions to American culture, Dave received the Kennedy Center Honors in 2012. The rainbow ribbon has three engraved medal bars — Kennedy Center Honor, David Letterman, and December 2, 2021.

After Dave retired from The Late Show in 2015, he donated many personal items to his alma mater Ball State University. His Kennedy Center Honor is now part of the David Letterman Collection at Bracken Library.

The Kennedy Center Honor is presented over a weekend-long ceremony. The event includes the Chairman's Luncheon, State Department dinner, White House reception, and the taped Honors gala performances followed by a dinner.

Dave laughs alongside fellow Kennedy Center Honors recipients, ballerina Natalia Makarova and actor Dustin Hoffman, at the White House reception in 2012.

Dave wasn’t the first late-night talk show host honored by the Kennedy Center. In fact, Dave’s idol and mentor, Johnny Carson, received the honors in 1993, and Dave was on hand to help present the award.

“A couple of months after that Johnny was nice enough to offer me a position as guest host on the rare occasion when he would take a night off. And there is nothing that I am today any more proud of than my association with Johnny Carson.” (2)

"In 1975 I moved from Indianapolis and went to Los Angeles to pursue a career in standup comedy and one of my secret dreams was to be a guest on the Tonight Show with Johnny Carson. I went on that show in 1978 and it was and still is the biggest thrill of my professional life."

- David Letterman at the 1993 Kennedy Center Honors ceremony (2)

Dave during one of his 22 appearances on The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson. Dave’s first appearance was in 1978. Dave was one of the few lucky comedians that Carson asked to come over to the interview set after he performed his stand-up routine. The invitation from Carson was highly sought after by comedians and signaled that Carson thought you were very funny.

"I would guess that maybe three generations of comedians moved to be where Johnny was because if you thought you were funny and you wanted to find out if you could hit major league pitching, you had to be on The Tonight Show with David Letterman after Carson's death"

- David Letterman 2005 during a tribute on Late Night (2)

Dave’s absurdist humor built on Carson’s talk show format; but in many ways, Dave was a pioneer. From Stupid Pet Tricks to his iconic Top-Ten lists, Dave influenced many hosts and comedians that came after him, including Jimmy Kimmel and Ray Romano.

“I know you hate all of this. But if you believe one thing, believe that what Johnny Carson was for you, you are for the rest of all of us here.”

- Ray Romano (3)

“Every night I wanted to be David Letterman. All of my friends wanted to be David Letterman. Ironically, the only person who didn’t want to be David Letterman, was David Letterman.”

- Jimmy Kimmel (3)

2012 Kennedy Center Honorees

Led Zeppelin

Formed in 1968, British band Led Zeppelin (John Paul Jones, Jimmy Page, Robert Plant and John Bonham) was a pioneer in transforming rock and roll into a heavy metal sound while still drawing inspiration from blues music. Their iconic song, Stairway to Heaven, is often called one of the greatest rock hits of all time.

Dustin Hoffman

Appearing in over 70 movies, Hoffman is a versatile movie actor who is often cast playing emotionally vulnerable characters. His most notable movies include: The Graduate, All the President’s Men, Krammer vs Krammer, Tootsie, and Rainman.

Natalia Makarova

A prima ballerina and choreographer, Makarova is considered one of the greatest classical dancers. She defected from Russia while on tour in London in 1970 and is best known for her principal role in the romantic ballet Giselle.

Buddy Guy

The son of sharecroppers, Guy joined the Chicago blues music scene with many other transplanted southern blues artists in the late 1950s. His legendary blues guitar playing influenced Jimi Hendrix, Stevie Ray Vaughan, and Eric Clapton.
